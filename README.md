### Personal Ethics Statement:
To start, I fully agree with Blackman's point that ethical guidelines should be very direct. Vagueness only serves to obfuscate true intention and avoid responsibility.<br>

I think that a code of ethics written properly is effectively a specification document. If it doesn't help an employee make a difficult choice between two competing priorities, it hasn't done its job.<br>

Now, past the philosophy that guides my response, here's the meat and potatoes: it's a bit tough to place an exact framework for ethical handling of data, given that my thoughts are that they should be as specific as possible. The question doesn't come with the pretense of a particular company with an expressly defined goal or product, so my only choice here would frankly be to deliver some bland and general virtues wrapped neatly with a bow.<br>

Put simply, any vague mumbo-jumbo I could throw at you right now would really just fly right in the face of the entire principle that upholds both Blackman's, and my own views on the subject.<br>

I know that being difficult with a non-answer is probably annoying (and I also acknowledge that I do this more often than not), but I do think that this very specific case for a non-answer is more thoughtful and intentional than any meaningless buzzword-filled alternative. I hope you would agree with that sentiment.<br>
